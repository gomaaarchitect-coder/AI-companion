Teaching Identity Interview

Live activity — take your time with this one

Example Prompt

Run a structured interview with Claude about your teaching

Conduct a teaching identity interview with me. Your goal is to help me articulate my teaching vision, and get to know me well enough to support me in future sessions. Rules: • One question at a time. • Probe each answer before moving on — push back if something is vague or contradictory, ask for examples, go deeper if something is interesting. • Keep your questions short. • Cover these four areas in order: 1. My core teaching values and philosophy 2. The unique constraints I face 3. The teaching methods I most value and why 4. My research focus and what I struggle with in it When the interview is complete: Produce a structured Teaching & Research Profile, including no less than two tensions I need to work on: [something that emerged from our conversation that I hadn't fully articulated]
💡 This is a live conversation — let Claude ask you questions one at a time. Push back on vague answers the same way you'd expect a good mentor to. The profile Claude produces at the end is yours to keep and build on.

Advanced Prompting

Stacked Persona

Combine multiple expert perspectives into one prompt

Example Prompt (multi-part)

Design a facilitated session using three simultaneous expert lenses

Expert 1 — Content Specialist
You are a senior _______ specializing in ________, with deep familiarity with __________.
Expert 2 — Pedagogical Facilitator
An experienced ____________________ who uses ________________ to draw out student thinking rather than transmit your own conclusions.
Expert 3 — Course Design Specialist
An expert in course facilitation design for young students in the field of [].
Task
Design a _______ for a group of [first-year undergraduates] in the field of ___: • Opens with a provocation that creates genuine intellectual discomfort (not a question students can answer from common sense alone) • Uses at least three primary source extracts • Includes a structured activity that forces students to take a position and defend it • Explicitly builds ONE ___________ [thinking] skill Do not write a lecture. Write a facilitation guide. Every instruction should be addressed to the instructor, not the student.
Synthesis Instruction
Facilitate the requirements of the three experts to create a cohesive ____________________.
💡 The key move: ask Claude to hold three expert viewpoints simultaneously and let them negotiate the output. The synthesis instruction at the end activates this — don't skip it.

— Expert 2: e.g. a methodology specialist, a journal editor, a statistician
— Expert 3: e.g. a grant reviewer, or a pedagogy expert if it's an educational study

Advanced Prompting

Chain of Thought (CoT)

Force Claude to reason step-by-step before it recommends anything

Example Prompt — Redesigning an AI-vulnerable assignment

How should I redesign my [essay] assignment now that students have access to AI?

Setup
I need to redesign a [second-year undergraduate research assignment] for a module on [environmental policy]. The current assignment is: "_____________________________________________" Before giving me any recommendations, work through this problem step by step:
Step 1 — AI Vulnerability Analysis
Analyze the current assignment for AI risk level (High / Medium / Low) and explain why.
Step 2 — Learning Outcome Audit
List the actual learning outcomes this assignment is designed to achieve. Then identify which of those outcomes require genuine student cognition and which could be satisfied by a well-prompted AI.
Step 3 — Redesign Options
Propose THREE different redesign approaches: • Option A: AI-Resilient (minimal AI involvement possible) • Option B: AI-Integrated (students use AI explicitly as part of the task) • Option C: AI-Transparent (students use AI freely but must declare its use)
Step 4 — Recommendation
Which option do you recommend and why?
💡 The phrase "before giving me any recommendations, work through this step by step" is the key. It stops Claude from jumping to solutions and forces genuine analysis first.
Your Task

Redesign my assignment on [] to be AI-proof — using Chain of Thought

Redesign my assignment on [] to be AI proof.
Your task: Take a real assignment from your own course. Fill in the blanks in the CoT example above — replace the placeholders with your actual course level, module topic, and current assignment description. Then run the full 4-step version.

Pay close attention to Step 2 — the Learning Outcome Audit often reveals that parts of an assignment were already superficial long before AI existed.

Advanced Prompting

Negative Prompting

Define what Claude must NOT do — to force better thinking

Example Prompt — Peer review without rewriting

Give feedback that makes students think — not copy

Context
This is an academic paragraph [written by a third-year undergraduate for a research methods assignment].
Constraints — Absolute Rules
Do NOT be agreeable. Do NOT rewrite, rephrase, or edit any part of the text. Do NOT suggest alternative sentences. Do NOT identify spelling or grammar errors.
Your Task
Act as a thoughtful peer reviewer who helps the writer improve their own work. You are permitted only to: • Ask questions about what the writer was trying to do (intention questions) • Identify moments where you, as a reader, became confused or lost • Name all the strengths in the paragraph The writer should leave this interaction knowing exactly what to revise and why — and should have arrived at that understanding themselves.
💡 Negative constraints are one of the most powerful prompting tools. By telling Claude what it cannot do, you force it to do the harder, more useful thing. The student learns more because Claude does less.
Your Task

Refine the language of this paragraph [] — using Negative Prompting

Refine the language of this paragraph []
Your task: Take a paragraph from student work (or your own writing) and run the bare prompt above first — note what you get. Then apply the Negative Prompting technique:

1. Set the context — who wrote it, for what purpose?
2. Write at least 3 clear constraints (things Claude must NOT do)
3. Define exactly what Claude IS permitted to do

Think carefully about what constraints would produce the most educationally useful feedback for your students' level and discipline.

Excel & Gen AI — Analysis
Example Prompt — Student evaluation analysis

Extract actionable insights from course evaluation data

In the attached file, analyze the [] sheet. This contains student evaluation scores (1–5 scale) for courses across 5 semesters and several departments. Act as an academic quality analyst and answer: 1. Which departments have the highest and lowest average overall satisfaction (Q5) across all semesters? 2. Is there any department where scores are consistently declining semester over semester? Any that are improving? 3. Which specific courses are underperforming (average Q5 below 3.0 across the semesters)? 4. Is there a relationship between student response rate (Responses / Enrolled) and the satisfaction scores? 5. Across all courses, which of the five questions (Q1–Q5) tends to score lowest? What might that suggest about where the faculty should focus improvement efforts? For each finding, give me the specific numbers, not just general statements. End with a short "Key Takeaways" section.
⚠️ Always verify key numbers against the raw data. Claude can misread column structures in complex spreadsheets. Use it for speed and pattern-spotting — then confirm the critical figures yourself.
Adjust Paper to Fit a Journal
Example Prompt — Full journal alignment workflow

Get Claude to analyse the journal before it analyses your paper

Step 1 — Analyse the Journal
For the journal [journal name], find no less than 10 recent articles and analyse them for common characteristics: document structure, common methodologies, accepted case studies, and common traits that you find.
Step 2 — Compare Your Paper
Then compare my article [upload your paper] to the common characteristics of the journal and produce a detailed report showing all the changes I must do to meet the journal's norms. The comparison should be based solely on the analysis of the recent journal papers. Do not use your training data or the journal's website aims and scope.
Step 3 — Prioritise & Schedule
List the required changes in order from most important to least important. Then create a detailed revision schedule for me to follow.
Step 4 — Honest Fit Assessment
Be honest: if my article is not a good fit for critical reasons (different scope, methods never used in the journal) — state your judgment with complete referenced reasons.
⚠️ Critical caution: Claude can fabricate citations. Never submit journal references it generates without manually verifying each one. Use this workflow for structure and strategy — not for your reference list.
Your Task

Fit your paper to a target journal

Use a paper you are currently working on or planning to submit. Work through the four-step prompt above, substituting:

— [journal name] → a journal you are genuinely targeting
— [upload your paper] → paste your abstract + introduction + methodology, or the full text

What to check in the output:
✔ Does the structure Claude recommends match what you see in recent issues?
✔ Are the methodological norms it identifies accurate for your field?
✔ Does the honest fit assessment flag anything surprising?

After the session: Take the revision schedule and verify it against 3 actual recent papers from that journal before acting on it.

Advanced AI for Students' Experiences

Assessment Design & Iterative Refinement

How to redesign what students submit — and how to build teaching sessions iteratively with Claude

Assessment for Gen AI
Example — Before: the unmodified assignment brief

What you currently give students

Write a report/essay on …
⚠️ In the age of Gen AI, this can be completed almost entirely by an LLM with zero intellectual engagement from the student. The redesigned version below adds accountability layers without banning AI.
Example — After: AI-Transparent Assessment Design

The redesigned assignment brief (to give to your students)

Write a 1,500-word report/essay on …… Submit alongside it: (a) A 500-word 'AI Consultation Log' documenting two interactions you had with the LLM during the research/drafting process, including — in table form — the prompts you used, what the AI produced, and why you accepted, rejected, or modified the output. (b) [Subject to student level] A 500-word 'Argument Ownership Statement' explaining one interpretive choice in the essay/report that the AI did not suggest and that you can defend verbally in the oral component. Integrity mechanism: 15-minute oral examination in the following week.
💡 The oral examination is the integrity mechanism. A student who outsourced their report cannot defend an argument they didn't form. The AI Consultation Log also teaches metacognitive awareness — students must evaluate AI output rather than just accept it.
Iterative Refinement
Example Prompt — 5-Loop Iterative Refinement

Build a teaching session across 5 iterative loops — think first, then refine

Opening Instruction
Break your teaching task into [5] iterative refinement loops.
Iteration 1 — Define + Ask
Define the teaching session + task, then ask me [3] questions that you need answered to refine the lecture.
Iteration 2 — Design
[After I provide answers:] Design the activity for maximum student engagement — include detailed facilitator instructions.
Iteration 3 — Challenge
Challenge and critique the proposal, then revise [only these points, and leave everything else unchanged] / [the entire activity — your choice].
Iteration 4 — Scaffold
Build a scaffold where the teaching session progresses from basic to advanced concepts.
Iteration 5 — Reflect
Reflect on what we just did, and write one paragraph on the session design including the design rationale and the pedagogical logic.
💡 The key habit: think first, then let AI help you develop your ideas. Before Iteration 1, write a rough outline of what you want the session to achieve — then let Claude help you build it.
Your Task

Refine one of your teaching activities — 6 loops

Pick one teaching activity you already use — a lecture segment, a group task, a discussion prompt, a lab activity. It doesn't need to be polished.

Before you start: Write 3–5 sentences about what you want students to learn and what's not currently working well. Do this before you open Claude.

Then run 6 iterative loops:

Loop 1 → Define + 3 questions
Loop 2 → Design with facilitator detail
Loop 3 → Critique + selective or full revision
Loop 4 → Scaffold basic → advanced
Loop 5 → Add one assessment moment
Loop 6 → Reflect on the pedagogical logic

At the end, compare your original 3–5 sentences to what Claude produced. What did you not think of? What did Claude miss or get wrong?
